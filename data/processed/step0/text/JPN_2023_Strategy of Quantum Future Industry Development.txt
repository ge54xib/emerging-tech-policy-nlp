# 1. Introduction

- ✓ The "Quantum Future Society Vision"(Integrated Innovation Strategy Promotion Council, April 22, 2022) outlines a vision for achieving a sustainable society, economy, and environment through the utilization of quantum technology. It sets three goals: increasing the number of domestic quantum technology users to 10 million by 2030, achieving a production value of 50 trillion yen through quantum technology, and creating quantum unicorn venture companies that pioneer future markets.
- ✓ To achieve these goals, it is crucial to advance the practical application and industrialization of quantum technology, linking it to value creation such as productivity enhancement, new industry creation, and solving societal challenges. Particularly, to realize the targeted number of users and production value, it is vital for diverse industries to utilize quantum technology in their own businesses and services, thereby forming a broad-based market.
- ✓ To promote the adoption of quantum technology across diverse industries, it is vital to focus on creating valuable use cases tailored to each sector. Examples include use cases in industrial fields (e.g., materials, chemistry, finance, health/medical, manufacturing, logistics/transportation, AI, information and communications) and addressing societal challenges (e.g., environment, water, food, energy, urban development, defense).Furthermore, actively disseminating information about the outcomes and value of these use cases is vital, as is creating an environment where executives and others understand this value and can utilize quantum technology in their business activities. Additionally, establishing an environment where diverse industries can effectively utilize quantum computers andrelatedtechnologiesiscrucial.
- ✓ The value market created by the utilization of quantum technology is expected to reach enormous scale in the future. Consequently, overseas, public and private investment is intensifying, and private companies are actively and rapidly expanding globally, leading to fierce international competition. Furthermore, as this is an industry requiring advanced technical capabilities, many private companies are developing businesses in close collaboration with universities and research institutions. This represents an area where basic research and industrial application are becoming closer than ever before.
- ✓ Therefore, for practical application and industrialization, it is crucial for industry, academia, and government to unite. While conducting fundamental research, they must strategically consider future business development from an early stage, with an eye toward global collaboration and expansion, accelerating efforts more than ever before.
- ✓ In emerging markets where quantum technology creates new value, startups and venture companies pioneering unprecedented new businesses and services can become key players. On the other hand, since much quantum technology requires long-term investment before practical application and industrialization, it is vital to form a venture ecosystem by attracting investment and talent to startups, etc., with a long-term perspective, taking into account the market characteristics of quantum technology fields requiring sustained investment.
- ✓
Inthefuture,astechnologieslikeAI,Beyond 5G, and semiconductors undergo rapid innovation and markets evolve at a dizzying pace, combining these with quantum technology is expected to drive socioeconomic development.
Furthermore, efforts toward realizing a sustainable socio-economic system, including SDGs and a decarbonized society, will be demanded more than ever, and quantum technology is expected to make significant contributions. When pursuing the practical application and industrialization of quantum technology, it is also important to maintain a perspective that flexibly responds to such technological innovations and future changes in the economy and society.
- ✓ Based on these considerations, and to achieve the goals outlined in the Quantum Future Society Vision, this report presents the policies to be pursued and the specific, priority initiatives to be undertaken for the practical application and industrialization of quantum technology, under collaboration between industry, academia, and government.

# 2. Positioning of This Report

The Quantum Future Society Vision complements the "Quantum Technology Innovation Strategy" (January 21, 2020, Integrated Innovation Strategy Promotion Council)(essentially a "Quantum Technology R&D Strategy"). In contrast, the Quantum Future Society Vision was formulated asa"Strategy for Societal Transformation through Quantum Technology"—outlining the future society envisioned through quantum technology and the strategy for its realization—to leverage quantum technology for creating growth opportunities for Japanese industry, solving societal challenges, and achieving transformation across society as a whole.
This report is formulated as a strategy outlining the policies and action plans for the practical application and industrialization of quantum technology. It aims to achievethe goals setforthinthe Quantum Future Society Vision through collaboration among industry, academia, and government, and specifies the direction to pursue for the practical application and industrialization of quantum technology, along with concrete initiatives that should be prioritized and focused on in the immediate term. In essence, it is a strategy presenting the policies and action plans for the practical application and industrialization of quantum technology.

# 5. Major Industrial Challenges and Basic Response Policies

## (1) Lack of compelling use cases to drive entry into the quantum technology field

### (Challenge)

- ✓ In quantum computing, quantum measurement/sensing, and quantum security/networks, there are currently few effective usecases demonstrating the benefits of quantumtechnology.
- ✓ User companies are particularly interested in the extent of benefits quantum technology offers compared to existing technologies, including performance, cost, and convenience. However, as the technology is still developing, there are few use cases that clearly demonstrate its advantages over existing technologies.
- ✓ Furthermore, there is a lack of accurate information—including current status and future outlook—regarding the benefits of utilizing quantum technology (such as advantages from a business perspective for user companies), which is essential for user companies to make commercialization decisions.

### (Basic Response Policy)

- ✓ Looking ahead to future quantum technology development, we will actively promote participation from various user industries to broaden the market base. We will provide support and disseminate information to create compelling use cases for users.When developing use cases, it is necessary to establish a framework for co-creation where diverse user companies, vendor companies, universities, research institutions, etc., exchange information and opinions and engage in personnel exchanges. Therefore, we will build a framework where industry, academia, and government can collaborate to develop use cases, taking into account areas of cooperation and competition as well as the characteristics of each use case.
- ✓ Furthermore, we will set precise benchmarks for the effects of utilizing quantum technology, including not only performance but also advantages and effectiveness over existing technologies in terms of cost, convenience, and other factors, from a management perspective. We will widely disseminate this information.In doing so, we will provide information such as Technology Readiness Leve l (TRL)and Business Readiness Level (BRL) necessary for users to make commercialization decisions, taking into account the current status and future outlook, including the fact that technologiessuch as quantumcomputers are still underdevelopment.
- ✓ Furthermore, for users, quantum technology is merely one means among others and is not viewed as particularly special. Therefore, it is also important to incorporate quantum technology into conventional (classical) technology systems (including migration from classical to quantum technology), advance the development of use cases within quantum-classical hybrid systems, and conduct benchmark comparisons with existing technologies to clearly demonstrate the benefits of utilizing quantum technology.

## (2) High Technical Barriers to Quantum Technology

### (Challenges)

- ✓ Commercializing and industrializing quantum technology often requires advanced knowledge and skills, leading companies outside the quantum technology field to perceive high barriers to entry.
- ✓ On the software side, participation from user companies across diverse fields is expected. However, many companies lack the necessary infrastructure (e.g., equipment) to utilize quantum technology and also lack expertise and personnel in this area.
- ✓ Regarding hardware, participation from stakeholders in conventional (classical) devices, components, materials, electronics, etc., is essential. However, information is often lacking on where entry opportunities and market potential exist.

### (Basic Policy)

- ✓ To encourage broad participation from industry in quantum technology, we will establish environments enabling user companies across diverse fields to utilize quantum technology (e.g., quantum computers, quantum measurement/sensing, quantum security/networks).
- ✓ Furthermore, since industries outside the quantum technology field often lack expertise and personnel in quantum technology, we will support their participation in terms of information, technology, and personnel. This will be achieved by expanding opportunities for exchange and collaboration with researchers and engineers in the quantum technology field, while also actively providing educational programs.
- ✓ To advance the practical application and industrialization of quantum technologies in the future, we will clarify and organize the necessary conventional (classical) devices, components, materials, electronics, and information and communication technologies. We will provide information on areas where new entry opportunities and market potential exist.

## (3) Future technology and markets are uncertain, and business risks are high

### (Challenges)

- ✓ Although the quantum technology industry is expected to have a massive market in the future, the winning strategies for technology and industry are not yet determined at this stage. This makes it difficult to forecast future technology and market trends. Furthermore, the need for long-term investment means that, compared to other industrial sectors, this field currently carries higher business risks.
- ✓ Particularly in Japan's industrial sector, there is a tendency for investment to be low in fields requiring such long-term investment.
- ✓ Furthermore, amid the difficulty in forecasting future technology and markets, some argue that individual companies undertaking highly uncertain basic research or investing in massive manufacturing facilities carries significant risk.

### (Basic Policy)

- ✓ To mitigate these business risks as much as possible, the public and private sectors will collaborate to establish shared prototyping, testing, and evaluation facilities accessible to multiple companies, building mechanisms for the effective and efficient utilization of public and private resources.
- ✓ Additionally, we will support the establishment and activities of open innovation frameworks where multiple companies collaborate in co-creation domains (such as common components).
- ✓ Even at the basic research stage, the government will actively provide necessary support, clarifying the division of roles between public and private sectors while responding to industrial needs.

## (4) Insufficient Environment for Startup/Venture Creation and Growth

### (Challenge)

- ✓ In emerging markets like the quantum industry, startups/ventures (including new business divisions of existing companies and carve-out ventures) pioneering unprecedented businesses and services can become key players.
- ✓ Overseas, numerous startups have emerged in both software and hardware sectors, actively raising substantial funds from VCs and others. Conversely, for startups with limited capital, quantum technology businesses requiring long-term investment, technological development, and market expansion—essentially a long-term battle—represent a high-risk area under private-sector market mechanisms alone.
- ✓ Furthermore, within Japan's investment environment, there are concerns that the total investment amount directed toward startups is insufficient, and that financial institutions tend to avoid investments with long payback periods. This creates an inadequate business environment and insufficient opportunities for the creation and growth of startups. Additionally, there are opinions that startups lacking sufficient capital, organizational structure, and information dissemination capabilities may face difficulties in smoothly expanding overseas.

### (Basic Policy)

- ✓ As the quantum technology field requires long-term investment and technological development, the government will actively use national projects as catalysts to encourage private investment. Through integrated support from industry, academia, and government, it will foster the creation and nurturing of startupsand other entities with a long-term perspective.
- ✓ We will promote the creation and support of startups through matching with financial institutions and partner companies, cultivate young entrepreneurial talent to become key players, and establish mechanisms to generate business ideas utilizing quantum technology (e.g., pitch contests, ideathons/hackathons).
- ✓
Furthermore, we will form an innovation foundation that comprehensively supports startups and other entities by fostering collaboration between existing companies and venture companies, collaboration among venture companies themselves, and ecosystem formation.
.

## (5) Shortage of Industrial Talent

### (Challenge)

- ✓ Globally, there is a shortage of industrial talent in the quantum technology field, and competition for talent is intensifying. To commercialize and industrialize quantum technology, it requires not only research and technical personnel in quantum technology but also talent from diverse technical fields (devices/components/materials, electronics, semiconductors, architecture/systems, software, information and communications, etc.).
- ✓ Furthermore, it is crucial to cultivate and secure not only business personnel supporting commercialization and industrialization (such as management, intellectual property, and legal experts) but also personnel in user fields.
- ✓ Bothvendorandusercompaniesfaceseveretalentshortages,making it necessary to cultivate and secure industrial talent in the quantum technology field in a long-term and strategic manner.

### (Basic Response Policy)

- ✓ Industry and academia, including universities, research institutions, and industry, will work together to develop and provide educational programs for various fields, including industrial personnel and students. Furthermore, we will promote personnel exchange and mobility among industry, academia, and government, both domestically and internationally, including talent matching between industry and academia (e.g., internship programs) and cross-disciplinary personnel exchanges.
- ✓ Furthermore, national R&D projects will actively establish mechanisms and set themes that enable participation from personnel across diverse fields.
- ✓ Furthermore, we will enhance and strengthen outreach activities, such as educational programs and science museum exhibitions, targeting younger generations in junior high and high schools, who are the source of industrial talent.

# 7. In Conclusion

The quantum industry is an industrial field requiring the combined efforts of various sectors—from hardware to software and services—and can be described as a comprehensive discipline. Therefore, it is crucial for organizations and talent from industry, academia, and government across diverse fields to collaborate closely and co-create new value.
Furthermore, giventhe involvementof numerousindustrialsectors,this is an area with extremely high potentialfor economic ripple effects across related industries. Considering this, quantum technology's growth can be expected to serve as a catalyst for realizing Japan's comprehensive growth.
A massive market for the quantum industry is anticipated in the future, and investment is intensifying globally. However, technology often experiences waves of investment enthusiasm (booms), and there may be periods of lower investment activity ahead. Therefore, it is crucial that industry, academia, and government strengthen their unity now, avoid reacting to every upswing and downswing, and instead steadily and continuously nurture technology and talent from a long-term perspective, working towards future success.Furthermore, from the perspective of attracting investment and talent in a sustainable manner, it is vital to strive for accurate information dissemination based on scientific evidence.
As quantum technology is an emerging market, it is vital to build an environment where young talent can start businesses and boldly pursue new ventures with free thinking. Furthermore, within existing companies, it is expected that management will take the lead in creating environments where young talent can challenge themselves asintrapreneursin newbusinessventures.
Looking ahead, rapid changes in technologies like AI, Beyond 5G, and semiconductors, alongside markets, are anticipated. Initiatives for sustainable socio-economic development, such as the SDGs and a decarbonized society, will be demanded more than ever. When practicalizing and industrializing quantum technology, a perspective that flexibly adapts to future technological innovations and socioeconomic changes is also vital.
Approximately 100 years after its inception, quantum technology is transitioning into a phase of full-scale practical application and industrialization. It is hoped that industries will leverage quantum technology to identify winning strategies for futuregrowth and enrich economic and social systems.
